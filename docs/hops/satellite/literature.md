# Hop-Specific Remote Sensing Literature (2020–2026): Methods, Sensors, and Honest Field-Scale Accuracy

> Teil der Satelliten-Recherche (Deep-Research-Schwarm, 2026-06-28, Workflow `satellite-hops-research`).
> Facette: `literature`. Übersicht & Empfehlung: `README.md`. Geometrie-Vorabbefund: `field-scale-backtest.md`.

**Zusammenfassung:** Peer-reviewed remote sensing work specific to hops is thin but converging, and almost all of it is European (Czech Hop Research Institute Žatec + CZU Prague; Slovenian IHPS) using UAV multispectral imagery, not satellite. The strongest recent result (Kumhálová et al., Remote Sensing 2025) shows UAV vegetation indices explain hop yield up to ~61% (R²≈0.6) but quality/alpha-acid only ≤18%, with best results late in the season. Satellite (Sentinel-2, 10 m) appears only as a regional companion to UAV in hop studies and is fundamentally mismatched to the 7 m vertical trellis with wide rows — heavy soil/shadow/canopy mixing means satellite is honestly regional screening (classification, phenology, anomaly flags), not within-field precision. Hop-specific disease (Peronospora), nitrogen, and LiDAR-structure remote sensing are essentially absent in the literature, so the best evidence comes from trellised analogues (vineyards) — clearly flagged below. For field precision at 0.5–2 ha hop scale, only UAV-borne sensors (cm–dm GSD) currently deliver, and even those are constrained by the vertical canopy geometry.

**Feldskalen-Verdikt:** Honest verdict: at 0.5–2 ha hop fields on a 7 m trellis, ONLY UAV-borne sensors (cm–dm GSD multispectral, ideally + thermal) are field- and sub-field-precise, and even they are constrained by the vertical canopy and wide rows — the one published hop yield model tops out at R²≈0.6 (yield) and ≤18% (alpha quality), best only late-season, and requires explicit soil/shadow masking. Satellite (Sentinel-2 10 m, Landsat 30 m) is honestly REGIONAL SCREENING for hops: usable for crop-type classification, phenology/green-up, and season anomaly flags (a 1–2 ha field is only ~5–20 clean, heavily mixed pixels), but NOT for within-field precision. No peer-reviewed hop-specific satellite precision result exists; disease/N/structure RS for hops is unproven and currently borrowable only from vineyard analogues.

**Infrastruktur-Implikationen:** Two distinct tiers are required, not one. (1) Satellite screening tier: ingest Copernicus Sentinel-2 L2A (BOA reflectance, 10/20 m), with cloud/shadow masking, per-parcel zonal statistics, and VI time-series (NDVI/NDRE/etc.) stored per field for phenology and anomaly detection — cheap, automatable, but explicitly labeled "regional screening" in the UI per project guardrails. (2) UAV precision tier ("Feld-Check"): this is the only path to field precision, so DoldenBlick needs an ingest/upload pipeline for grower- or service-flown UAV multispectral (5-band) and optionally thermal orthomosaics — including orthomosaic storage, GSD metadata, soil/shadow (binary canopy) masking, and zonal/sub-field VI extraction. Modeling: random-forest/regression yield-vigor models that are growth-stage-aware (weight late-season captures), with honest accuracy caveats surfaced (no quality/alpha claims). Storage should be per-field time-series of VIs from BOTH tiers so satellite anomalies can trigger targeted UAV flights. Compute is modest (zonal stats, light ML); the real cost is the UAV data-acquisition workflow and calibration, not the cloud pipeline.

## Scope and headline finding

Hop-specific (Humulus lupulus) satellite/UAV remote-sensing literature is small, recent, and dominated by **two Central-European groups**: the Czech Hop Research Institute (Chmelařský institut, Žatec; K. Krofta) with the Czech University of Life Sciences Prague (J. Kumhálová), and the Slovenian Institute of Hop Research and Brewing (IHPS, Žalec). US PNW (WSU/USDA-ARS) and German LfL/IPZ-Hüll work exists for agronomy and water balance but **almost no peer-reviewed *imagery-based* hop remote sensing** surfaced — a real gap worth noting for DoldenBlick. Nearly all credible field-precise results come from **UAV multispectral**, not satellite.

## Hop-specific UAV studies (the core evidence)

**1. Kumhálová, Chyba, Krofta, Starý, Brant (2025), *Remote Sensing* 17(6):970 — the most relevant paper.** Czech (CZU Prague + Hop Research Institute Žatec). Four consecutive seasons (2020–2023), selected cultivars under conventional and organic management. UAV multispectral camera; indices **NDVI, GNDVI, NDRE, CIR, SAVI** used as proxies for vitality/health/structure to predict yield and quality (alpha-acid content of cones).
- Accuracy: **spectral indices explained hop *yield* up to ~61% (R²≈0.61)**; **quality/alpha-acid only ≤18%** of variance. Results were **markedly better at later growth stages** (closer to harvest), once the bine canopy fills the trellis.
- Scale: plot/cultivar-management level (field-scale UAV); yield modeling is not robust at fine sub-field resolution and quality prediction is weak. Honest takeaway: UAV VIs are useful for *relative vigor/yield ranking*, poor for brewing quality.

**2. Kumhálová, Chyba, Krofta, Starý, Brant (2021), ISHS *Acta Horticulturae* 1328_13 — "Evaluation of UAV and Sentinel-2 images to estimate condition of hop plants."** senseFly **eBee X** fixed-wing UAV with three cameras (**RGB, S.O.D.A., Parrot Sequoia** multispectral) plus **Sentinel-2**. Five cultivars, organic vs conventional. NDVI plus RGB indices; a **binary green-leaf-vs-soil model** thresholded to isolate plant area — an explicit acknowledgement of the **soil/canopy mixing problem** central to hops.
- Findings: significant NDVI differences among cultivars and management systems. Crucially, "**green area derived from UAV images was influenced by flight parameters and crop structure**" — i.e., the trellis row geometry directly biases the signal and demands careful calibration. Sentinel-2 used as the regional companion layer, not for within-field detail.

These two are effectively the canonical hop-imagery references; both come from the same Czech group, which is the world's most active in this niche.

## Satellite for hops: honest resolution reality

No hop study found uses satellite as a *field-precise* instrument. The 10 m Sentinel-2 / 30 m Landsat pixel sits on a crop with **~3 m wide rows and a 7 m vertical trellis**, so every pixel mixes illuminated canopy, shadowed canopy, and bare/cover-crop soil — and the canopy is vertical, violating the flat-Lambertian assumption behind NDVI. Consequence: Sentinel-2 is credible for **crop-type classification, phenology/green-up timing, season-to-season anomaly flags, and regional screening** (Central-European crop classification with Sentinel-2 time series reaches >0.87 kappa, but that is for *parcels of typical field crops*, not within-hop-field precision). For a 0.5–2 ha hop field that is only ~5–20 clean Sentinel-2 pixels after edge/mixing loss — enough to say "this field looks behind/ahead of normal," not "this corner needs water/N." Treat satellite as **screening + context**, never as the field-precise layer.

## Water/ET and nitrogen — mostly agronomy, little imagery

- **Hop crop coefficients / ET** (Spain, 'Nugget'): basal Kcb ≈ **0.16 / 0.97 / 0.83** (initial/mid/end), with transpiration related to yield and alpha-acids (Fandiño et al. line of work). This is sap-flow/eddy/FAO-56 agronomy, not imagery — but directly feeds the kind of FAO-56 water-balance service DoldenBlick already runs.
- **Hallertau water balance** ("A new approach for predicting the water balance of hops"): gas-exchange-derived consumption **>50 L/plant/day** at peak, Penman-Monteith correction factors. Again ground/model-based, not remote sensing.
- **Nitrogen via UAV in hops: essentially no peer-reviewed studies found.** All UAV-N evidence is from cereals/rice/maize/vineyards.

## Disease (Peronospora) and structure (LiDAR) — gaps filled by analogues

- **Downy mildew / Peronospora**: no hop-specific UAV/satellite detection paper found. Closest analogues (flag as analogues): grapevine downy mildew (*Peronospora*) **early detection by UAV thermal imaging** (thermal outperformed multispectral/hyperspectral for pre-symptomatic stress), and opium-poppy *Peronospora* detection by UAV multispectral+thermal. Hops share the trellised, vertical-canopy geometry with vineyards, so these are the best proxies — but they are proxies.
- **Canopy structure / biomass via LiDAR**: no hop study. Analogues show UAV-LiDAR canopy height R²≈0.86 (dry bean) — promising for estimating bine fill on the trellis, but unproven for hops and complicated by the wire/trellis returns.
- **Vineyard UAV water stress** (Precision Agriculture, 2024): combined thermal + multispectral + 3D imagery for vine water status — the closest trellised-crop template for a future hop "Feld-Check."

## Climate/yield context (not imagery, but shapes the case)

Možný et al. (2023, *Nature Communications*): across Hallertau, Žatec, and Slovenian regions, post-1995 mean yield fell **~13.7% in Hallertau / ~10.5% in Žatec** and alpha-acid **~15.6% / ~10.5%**, with **4–18% yield and 20–31% alpha declines projected by 2050**. This is the strategic argument for monitoring vigor/water stress — but it is statistical/meteorological, not remote sensing.

## Bottom line for method design

If DoldenBlick wants a *field-precise* "Feld-Check," the evidence says **UAV multispectral (and ideally thermal) flown late-season**, with explicit soil/shadow unmixing (binary canopy masking as Kumhálová did), random-forest/regression VI models, and expectations capped at ~R²≈0.6 for yield and poor for quality. Satellite belongs in a **screening/anomaly tier**, not the precision tier. Most hop-specific signal beyond yield-vigor (N, disease, structure) is unproven and must be borrowed cautiously from vineyard analogues.
